About

Hello!

I'm Sara, and I primarily write personal narrative essays and short fiction.

I also love to create planning and organizational tools, sing, make art, teach, and advocate for Autistic people and those with chronic illness.

Presently I'm working on a collection of short stories while pitching some standalone brief fiction and essays to online and print publications. 

I also publish a sporadic email newsletter where subscribers receive musings, updates on projects, recommendations and more.

I live and work in Seattle, Washington, U.S. with my spouse and three kiddos.

From Consultant to Writer

I began writing even before I learned to read.

As a shy and often silent child, I turned to writing and visual arts as a means of self-expression. They were the simplest ways for me to communicate.

I've been writing professionally since 2006, and dabbled in various creative ventures after serving as Vice President of a small company.

In college, I started freelancing as a ghostwriter. After my first two children were born, I fell victim to a multi-level marketing scam. Yet, that experience revealed my passion for organization and design, which later led to my first company, a graphic design business called Goff Designs.

My design clients often struggled to market and grow their businesses on tight budgets. As someone who loves solving problems, I experimented with web design and discovered a passion for developing online growth strategies. I found that most clients needed more than just graphics; they required a full range of services.

With passion and perseverance, I designed strategic courses for entrepreneurs and still offer a business and content planner, which one loyal customer described as a "business coach in your bag".

However, in 2013, shortly after my youngest was born, life as I knew it became unmanageable.

Having three young kids when I had planned to have none was a factor, but it was only part of the struggle.

I experienced a decline in my mental health, and I grew sicker, larger, and less able to walk due to chronic, debilitating abdominal pain.

At the time, I shifted away from business writing and focused more on personal narratives to process what was happening to me.

What started as bullet journaling and writing daily summaries evolved into longer, handwritten journal entries.

Then, one day, it hit me: I have no idea what my body's doing. Countless doctors have no idea what's going on either. I can't be the only person who has ever faced a health problem that isn't easily solved; I should document and share my experience.

I began writing on Medium, sharing my experiences and memories in raw fragments.

It was cathartic.

For what felt like the first time in my conscious life, people listened to and engaged with me. I felt valuable, and like I was contributing something meaningful to society. This felt more rewarding than any positive feedback or earnings brought in from my design portfolio, products, professional projects, business articles, or any form of ghostwriting ever did.

”Intimate, personal writing and advocacy have given me the opportunity to help more people in a way that adds joy and hope into my own life, too.”

My Diagnostic Journey

I saw around 16 different medical doctors in the two years before a gastroenterologist finally diagnosed my debilitating abdominal pain as Celiac Disease. But only after another doctor had unnecessarily performed a total hysterectomy, thinking my pain was caused by a flipped uterus.

By then, I had turned my consulting business into an automated email course series. (One of my 2025 "hopeful" projects is to revive this as a standalone resource website for entrepreneurs.)

I stopped seeing clients and reduced my available products to just one planner for professionals.

In just over a year on Medium, I gained almost 1,000 regular readers of my personal essays and stories. That felt promising. 

After the celiac diagnosis, I immediately cut gluten out of my diet. I went from being unable to stand for more than a few minutes to being able to zip around the house. I tackled projects I only dreamed of getting to "if I made it out alive." I lost 100 pounds in a year, but more health issues emerged and worsened over time.

As far back as I can remember, I've been hypersensitive to sunlight and heat, but my reactions became so severe that I had to remain indoors at all times.

Even in the shade, my skin would flush, grow hives, bubble, and blister. I've likened the feeling to trying to "hug a bonfire" or trying to "make out with a flat-top grill."

Even gluten-free foods began causing my skin and other systems to overreact. I'd get severe stomach cramps and gut pain like fireworks exploding inside me. I had gastrointestinal issues, brain fog, fatigue, joint pain, and a racing heart. And with some foods and exposures, like heat, I experience dyspnea. Although I was mobile again, I still found myself stuck inside for most of the year, thinking that's how life would be from then on.

As a nature-loving person, it felt like a prison sentence.

After evacuating our home before Hurricane Irma in 2017, we noticed that the further I got from Florida, the better I could handle the sun. The lower humidity probably helped too. Now, with lots of meds, extra skin protection, a histamine-avoidant diet, and cooling devices, my systemic reactions are better under control. Except for heat, which only seems to get worse with time.

Feeling hopeful, when we returned home a week and a half later, we took steps to leave the state for good. After a month of research, we decided to vet Seattle as a potential new home. It has the second safest-for-me year-round climate in the U.S., behind the more expensive San Francisco. Plus, it could support my dietary and healthcare needs.

Fast-forward to mid-2018, our Florida house sold, and we officially made the cross-country move to Seattle.

Our first apartment was a third-floor walk-up. At first, I thought I was out of shape, but as time went on, getting up to the apartment became harder. I developed plantar fasciitis and Achilles tendon issues, knee and hip problems, and post-stair tachycardia. It felt like my body was on the verge of anaphylactic reactions from even minor activity. A couple of months before our lease was up, we left that apartment, opting for a place with air conditioning and elevators. But by late 2019, after a system-triggering attempt to go vegan, my body's multi-system reactivity exploded.

Over-75-degree days had me stuck indoors with the A/C blasting. Thankfully, that's only a few days in a Seattle summer. In Florida, I couldn't be outside for more than a couple of minutes for 50 out of 52 weeks of the year.

I couldn't breathe well. Climbing stairs was out of the question. And my body could only eat about a dozen foods. We had to thoroughly cook everything, and my system only tolerated very fresh meats or frozen vegetables. My body resumed "survival mode." It likely kept me alive for almost 30 years with undiagnosed celiac disease. But that meant I was gaining weight again.

Then, the COVID-19 pandemic took over everyone's lives in early 2020.

This began a privileged but stressful time of co-teaching my three young kids in online school for the next two-plus years. And, as you may guess, my health took a further nosedive.

After years of declining health, my symptoms are now more manageable. Although my tolerance to heat is worsening.

And in the wake of embracing a "health hiatus" for most of 2024, I took time to learn how to better cope with my chronic conditions. Along the way, I learned so much about myself, my predicament, and even more about my family. Unlike during the pandemic, I was able to be mindful of my family's and my own needs, and started to build healthier habits around life and work.

This time, I was able to make the choice to stop everything to focus on what matters.

Since, I've collected what I hope are my last diagnoses. With them acknowledged, accommodated, and better managed, I'm finally living my best life. I'm writing about it in personal stories and my newsletter musings. And exploring life through "what if" fictionalized scenarios.

For the fellow chronic illness sufferers or the curious, I've been officially diagnosed with (in order of diagnosis):

  • Anxiety
  • Celiac Disease
  • ADHD
  • Autism (ASC – Autism Spectrum Condition; formerly diagnosed with Bipolar II Disorder, Major Depressive Disorder and Borderline Personality Disorder, which are common Autism-in-women misdiagnoses)
  • c-PTSD
  • Mast Cell Activation Syndrome (MCAS)
  • Asthma
  • Hypermobile Ehlers-Danlos Syndrome (hEDS)
  • Thoracic Outlet Syndrome
  • Postural Orthostatic Tachycardia Syndrome (PoTS) / Chronic Orthostatic Intolerance (COI)
  • Labile Hypothyroidism (system dysregulated)
  • Labile Hypertension (when triggered repeatedly)
  • Inducible Laryngeal Obstruction (vocal cord dysfunction triggered by heat)
